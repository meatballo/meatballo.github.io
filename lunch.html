Monch

A lunch box for those new to packing their food.

The form is animated, simple, and intuitive to help mitigate the cognitive gap between not packing one's lunch at all to packing consistently. This project was different from the rest in that there were no due dates during the process; we were to discover our own.

Time

 Fall 2023, 5 weeks

Role

 ID, UX Research, CMF, Ergonomics

Tools

 SolidWorks, Keyshot, IdeaMaker, FDM Printer

An on-the-go meal experience.


Eating a meal away from home has been an universal experience for quite some time, but for the college student, the pathway from not packing one's food to packing everyday is a huge leap to take. With a rising demand for personalized experiences in traditional, uniform workplaces, this project aims to help the user turn packing meals into a habit or, eventually, an enjoyable ritual.

Looking at existing lunch carriers.


What works across cultures? Why? What were the reasons for each lunch carrier's design? For status, utility, ritualizing food, etc.?

I focused on the idea of ritualizing food. This can be found in small differences in how people pack their food: whether they throw their food all together or deliberately plate their meal.

How do I help someone change their behavior?


My biggest wrestle with this project was with how to help the user change their mindset.

Those who want to begin packing: their motivators are to save money, eat healthier, and gain independence.
Those who successfully pack regularly: their motivators are simply because they love cooking and packing their food. For them, preparing one's food is a ritual and joy.

Conducting interviews to understand mindset, and defining my project direction.


For scripting the interview, I broke it down through motions: packing food, transport, eating, cleaning.

Mindset of those who currently pack: looks forward to shopping, cooking, packing, eating... just not so much on cleaning (understandably).
Mindset of those who want to pack: are motivated by saving money, health, and view packing as a task/chore.
Mindset of those stopped: Most did not have enough time anymore, tupperware was too heavy, disliked cleaning, etc.
How can I make a product ‘promise’ consistency, saving money, eating healthier, enjoying cooking/packing, etc.?

With user mindset in mind, I ideated.


Thinking about utensils, modular compartments, comfort of eating at home, sizing with backpack, sizing with sponge, how to transport, etc.

I didn't want to design a lunch box that simply addressed all the pain points I discovered because a lot of users didn't like the idea of having too much in a 'kit' - it's a hassle. Instead, I focused on ergonomics, sizing for the backpack, cleaning, and simplicity.

Prototyping


I tested different sizes and shapes to see how it felt with the hand, if it fits in a bag, with a sponge, with a spoon, etc.

Narrowing down with sketching + modeling.


Thinking about how people like to hold their food while eating = a sense of comfort.

The silhouette of the form translates how it feels, so I moved into making the form feel animated. I also looked into what makes some forms feel ritualistic, and how can I balance mine to feel the same?

How can the surface appearance compliment the form?


I chose a bright reddish-orange because it's not too dull to feel dirty from food, but warm-feeling to match the form. The color placement indicates how the vacuum mechanism works, both the gasket and switch, so it's easier to understand.

The clear top allows the user to see their food, as well as to check the cleanliness of the gasket area. The body is opaque as most users did not like the appearance of food against glass.

Product Details


Monch includes a gasket lip and vent hole.

The gasket lip helps the user take out the gasket for easy cleaning. The lid is made of HDPE clear plastic to allow the user to see if there's any debris near the gasket.
The vent hole allows for a tight vacuum seal. The seal can be broken by pressing on the plastic button, and a silicone nub releases pressure.

Why the vent hole?


Most lunch boxes include clasps, which offer a secure, leak-proof seal. Through two rounds of interviews, I found that sound matters a lot - most users disliked the clasps due to the noise it makes in a room.

Going the vent hole route was both a response to my research and a personal challenge :)

Final considerations + thoughts.


This project affirmed my love for the user/person - it was fun for me to learn about different mindsets and habits, and how our everyday objects affect that. Given more time, I'd reexplore utensil storage and CMF.

In all, my main focus for this project became sizing to everyday things: fitting into the backpack, fitting a cleaning sponge, the curve of a spoon, and the hand.
As well as product promise: does the form promise comfort? Simplicity? Easy integration into routine?
