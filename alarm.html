Operation Room Fire Alarm


A concept fire alarm for an operation room - designed with a focus on interaction, usability, and context.

In an environment that requires quick, hands-free action, a fire alarm needs to be immediately noticed and understood.

Time

 4 weeks

Role

 ID, UX Research, Human Factors + Ergonomics

Tools

 SolidWorks, Keyshot, IdeaMaker, FDM Printer, Laser Cutter, Sauder

Why a specific fire alarm?


Operation room fires are rare, but deadly.

The oxygen tanks, personnel, and patient are all key factors to consider. In the case the fire gets out of control, the surgeons must prep the patient first, then rush out the OR.
Speed and easy accessibility are key in an operating room emergency.

Asking the professionals themselves.


I was lucky enough to speak with a couple people who’ve worked in an OR/hospital setting.

The main concern with OR fires is either putting the fire out or rushing the patient out as quickly as possible. This means an ideal spot for the alarm would be within the room, so that a team member can alert others of the situation.

Core Considerations


The alarm should be quick:

Low cognitive load allows for faster reaction time
A no-hand activation would free the person to perform other tasks
The alarm should be

recognizable

:
A contrasting form will help personnel locate the alarm
Lastly, the alarm should protect from accidental triggers while still being easily activated.

Exploring Interactions: Hand? Elbow? Knee?


I explored different potential interactions as I slowly narrowed down my concept.

For the hand, it would likely be occupied + is unconventional for an OR setting. OR’s have buttons for emergencies, rather than intricate pulls.
For the knee/foot, I was afraid that an alarm out of eyesight would be difficult to notice in an emergency.

Big form changes to smaller details.


After establishing my perimeters, I went down the ideation train - choo choo.

Here, I focused on visual cues + affordances as well as protecting from accidental triggering. I also did some exploration into what makes a button a button; such as door pushes, stop buttons, etc.

A couple of design choices.


The alarm’s tilt on the wall.
This tilt presents the alarm to the user, so that the fear of triggering an alarm is mitigated.

Alarm protective measures.
The jut out of the wall helps prevent placement of equipment in front of it, and includes a lip/guard at the base to stop accidental triggers.
Rather than a straight button, it’s a hinge.
This allows for the light placement, which signals to the user that the alarm is activated. Additionally, a hinge compliments the tilted form without compromising the action.
Finally, the scale.
A smaller, more conventional alarm suggested hand-usage rather than an elbow.

Prototype: first run with sauder + circuits.


The hinge and shell are friction-fit so that the button stays down after being pressed. I also added ribs within the model for some tactile feedback when the button’s pressed.

LEDs fit in clear acrylic so that the light is distributed across the alarm, and copper tape completes the circuit after pressing.

Final + Afterthoughts


I designed a big button.

This was a disappointing note at first, but given the amount of thought + small choices I made, I found it pretty fun. If given the time/chance, I’d speak more with professionals and visit an OR to better understand my context before designing.
