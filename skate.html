Hockey Skate Protection


A rather specific concept product that aids a researcher in inflating mouse lungs.

Done during the Vanderbilt(VI4) Art Residency, where artists/designers are paired with a researcher to define and create works. I figured creating a concept product would be fun, especially with a researcher, Kristy Chiang, in contact.
An ongoing personal project!

Time

 6+ weeks

Role

 ID, UX Research, Human Factors + Ergonomics

Tools

 SolidWorks, Keyshot

As we all know, inflating mouse lungs can be very annoying.


I began by asking Kristy what she struggles with in her workflow: mouse lungs.

“8/10 times, the lung inflation fails.”
That’s a lot of mice wasted, so we found that a couple core causes are:
the catheter tends to poke + break the trachea during insertion or inflation
the trachea is too short + slips off the catheter
the Styrofoam base she must work on is not even

Some further research.


Market Analysis
Most other products were for live mice. The price and bulkiness of the other products also proved an issue. As for other, lower-tech methods, pumping the lung via intratracheal installation is still best for preserving the lung. Yet, other intratracheal methods still involved a lot of equipment and moving parts.

A look into her current model.


Kristy’s current set up was on a Styrofoam base because the apparatus had to be pinned down. Additionally, her hand positioning for inflation is uncomfortable + unstable.

*she sent me videos and images of the set up, but I’ve sketched it out to avoid any triggering imagery :>

Broad Ideation


I found that stability was a major factor in punctured trachea; this stems from the Styrofoam base + the entire setup potentially moving.

Some concepts that keep the lung fixed in relation to the syringe, as well as considerations for better ergonomics.

Narrowing down the motion.


After Kristy gave feedback on the initial concepts, I found that the lung and syringe must be either vertical or horizontal - this complicated ergonomics a bit.

I settled on this motion, as it made the most sense in the entire mouse lung sequence. The others blocked the lung from sight or endangered the lung.

Prototypes for Proportions


Because ergonomics is key, getting the overall form down was important.

Form Development


With all the perimeters and motions established, I slowly developed the form to fit the lab environment. This involved looking into similar products (pipettes, syringes, etc.)

CAD Exploration


Along with sketching, CAD ideation helped with understanding proportions + relations.
